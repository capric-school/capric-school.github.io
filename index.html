The 1st Capri PhD School on Cyber Physical Cloud

Anacapri, Capri Island, Italy

October 13-17, 2025

Description

Welcome to Capri and welcome to the first edition of the PhD School on Cyber Physical Cloud!

We live in an era of tremendous transformation, where digital systems are progressively embedded in physical devices adopted in our daily life, from vehicles and household appliances to heavy machinery adopted in industrial automation.

Recently, many industrial and research initiatives are blooming, fostering the adoption of the cloud paradigm and related technologies in cyber physical systems, to both adhere to stringent size, weight and power requirements, while embracing the pay-per-use model typical of cloud providers. As a side effect, this shift allows to re-purpose in the embedded and real-time the vast expertise available in the cloud domain.

The PhD School has the objective of teaching both the fundamentals and practical aspects behind the concepts of cloud computing, real-time virtualization, and their application to build next generation cyber physical cloud systems.

Audience

The PhD school is aimed at PhD students, early-stage researchers, industry practitioners, and master's degree students who are finishing their theses and intend to apply for a PhD, who want to explore the blend of real-time and cloud computing and their applications on cyber physical systems.

Introduction to Real-Time Systems

Abstract. Real-time systems are computer systems that must not only compute correct values but must do so within strict timing constraints. Applications range from aircraft and industrial control to everyday devices like phones and consumer electronics. This lecture outlines the foundational principles of real-time computing, and why simply being "fast on average" is not sufficient for correctness.

Scheduling of Virtual Resources

Abstract. Scheduling, the allocation of resources in order to satisfy timing constraints, is a critical enabler for real-time computing. This lecture will cover the fundamentals of real-time scheduling in the context of cloud computing: how computing resources and the workloads that use them are modeled; how their timing capabilities and requirements are specified; and how the workloads are executed upon the resources in order to satisfy the specified requirements.

Bio. Sanjoy Baruah is the Hugo F. & Ina Champ Urbauer Professor of Computer Science and Engineering at Washington University in Saint Louis. His research interests and activities are in real-time and safety-critical system design, scheduling theory, and resource allocation.

Multicore and Hierarchical Scheduling

Abstract. Virtualization, running multiple virtual machines (VMs) or containers upon the same physical hardware in an apparently isolated manner, is central to cloud computing. By organizing resource allocation policies in a layered fashion, hierarchical scheduling allows for flexible, scalable, and fair resource sharing across different users, groups, or components in a system and thereby enables virtualization. In this lecture we will learn how hierarchical scheduling is implemented upon computing platforms that may comprise multiple processing units.

Bio. Sanjoy Baruah is the Hugo F. & Ina Champ Urbauer Professor of Computer Science and Engineering at Washington University in Saint Louis. His research interests and activities are in real-time and safety-critical system design, scheduling theory, and resource allocation.

Real-time Virtualization and Hypervisors

Real-time Virtualization and Hypervisors

Abstract. Virtualization is at the heart of cloud computing, as it allows to have multiple virtual machines (VMs) sharing the same hardware resources, improving hardware consolidation and utilization efficiency. Containers, as a form of OS-level lightweight virtualization, are widespread used too. But what are the implications for real-time services, on their timeliness and freedom from interferences from multiple VMs and/or containers sharing the same resources? In this lecture we will present the principles of virtualization and hypervisors and the main solutions adopted to deal with real-time constraints, including real-time schedulers in hypervisors, partitioning hypervisors, and real-time containers.

Bio. Marcello Cinque is Associate Professor at the University of Naples Federico II where he teaches operating systems and real-time systems. He holds a PhD in Computer Engineering earned from University of Naples Federico II in 2006, and he was visiting scholar at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. His research interests mainly focus on real-time virtualization, dependable computing and failure analysis. Heâ€™s co-author of over 100 peer-reviewed papers on these subjects. He is or has been the principal investigator of many funded research projects on dependable and real-time computing. He contributes actively to the research community as program committee chair for several top ranking conferences, including USENIX ATC and IEEE DSN.

Facing the Uncertainties of Reliable Distributed Industrial Applications

Facing the Uncertainties of Reliable Distributed Industrial Applications

Abstract. The increasing trend of distributing closed-loop systems, such as offloading control algorithms to the cloud, introduces significant challenges including communication delays and connection losses. Achieving reliable distributed applications critically depends on considering these effects during application design to maintain stability and functional performance. This lecture will outline the resulting challenges for classical industrial product development. Attendees will gain a foundational understanding of how to quantify the impact of timing uncertainties on cyber-physical cloud systems.

Bio. Kevin Schmidt is a technical expert and project manager at Bosch Research, focusing on the uncertainty-aware design and validation of safety-critical and distributed systems. Like the Bosch portfolio, his work spans embedded control systems to cloud-based distributed control.
